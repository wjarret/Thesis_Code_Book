Abstract:
Mapping Spliceosomal snRNA RNA-Protein Interaction Networks Using RNP-MaP RNA-protein interactions are central to nearly every aspect of gene regulation, governing processes such as RNA transcription and localization, ultimately shaping cell identity and fate. However, defining the organization and function of RNA-protein interaction networks remains a major challenge, particularly because many RNAs lack strong primary sequence conservation or well-defined structural annotation. Traditional approaches to study RNA-protein interactions, for example, RNA immunoprecipitation or reconstituted binding assays, often miss the dynamic and cooperative nature of Ribonucleoprotein (RNP) assemblies inside cells and are limited to the interactions of only a few proteins at a time. In contrast, RNP-MaP (Ribonucleoprotein Networks Analyzed by Mutational Profiling) measures interactions between multiple proteins on an RNA molecule inside cells. Here, we propose two aims, to determine whether RNP-MaP can detect how cellular perturbation alters RNPs, and can we apply RNP-MaP transcriptome-wide to identify RNPs from their interaction signatures. As proof-of-principle, we will investigate interactions of Sm-proteins and small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs), which together are central to spliceosomal function. Sm-associated small nuclear RNPs (snRNPs) assemble at a structurally conserved Sm binding site, forming well-defined complexes that provide an ideal system for characterizing RNP network formation. Our preliminary data reveal consistent RNP-MaP reactivity patterns at the Sm binding site across multiple snRNAs, providing a template for mapping interaction networks transcriptome-wide.
